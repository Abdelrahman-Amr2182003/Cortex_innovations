Vacuum assisted Brine Rejection

Jun 2020
brine rejection
clean water
water management
sustainable

Vacuum-assisted brine rejection harnesses the power of vacuum technology to expedite brine removal during desalination. The process starts by placing saline water in a vacuum chamber to create low pressure, which encourages the rapid freezing of water while separating it from brine. This environment accelerates the crystallization process, as the vacuum reduces the boiling point of water and removes excess moisture quickly. By limiting energy consumption compared to conventional desalination, this technique makes the freezing process faster and less costly. Moreover, it enhances the yield of pure freshwater by minimizing the likelihood of salt crystallization in the ice. As a result, vacuum-assisted brine rejection could be a game-changer for water-scarce regions by delivering efficient and cost-effective freshwater generation.Despite being a relatively new approach, it promises scalability and adaptability, helping to meet global water demand sustainably.

Centrifuge brine rejection

Mar 2021
brine rejection
clean water
sustainable
water management

Centrifuge brine rejection leverages the physics of centrifugal force to separate salt from water efficiently.
Saline water is introduced into a centrifuge, which rotates at high speeds to produce a force that pushes denser salt particles outward while allowing less dense pure water to move inward.
This separation technique is advantageous because it reduces the amount of energy required for desalination compared to standard methods like reverse osmosis or thermal distillation.
The process is scalable and versatile, adaptable to varying salt concentrations and water sources.
Additionally, this method minimizes waste, as it concentrates brine efficiently while capturing a higher percentage of drinkable water.
With its potential for automation, centrifuge brine rejection can be easily integrated into existing desalination plants or new, standalone systems.
Its innovative approach shows promise for widespread adoption in the near future.

Ice sweating concept

Dec 2020
ice sweating
clean water
sustainable
water management

Ice sweating is an environmentally friendly desalination method that mimics the natural freeze-thaw cycles.
By freezing saline water to form ice, pure water crystallizes, while most of the salt remains in the liquid phase.
As the ice slowly melts in a controlled environment, pure water droplets (or "sweat") form on the surface, leaving behind a concentrated brine.
This concept requires minimal energy input because it uses the natural phase change properties of water.
Although relatively simple, it can produce clean, drinkable water in regions with high salt concentrations or in isolated, off-grid areas where traditional desalination isn't feasible.
The controlled freezing ensures that the ice retains high purity, while the sweating process recovers freshwater efficiently.
This method has significant potential for providing fresh water at low operational costs, making it a valuable tool for sustainable water management.

Density Concept

Sep 2020
water management
clean water
sustainable

The density concept is a straightforward desalination method based on the natural differences in density between saltwater and freshwater.
In this process, saline water is carefully layered with freshwater to promote separation, using differences in salinity and temperature to facilitate density stratification.
The denser saltwater sinks, while the less dense freshwater floats, allowing for natural separation.
This separation is enhanced by controlling external parameters like temperature, pressure, and salinity gradients, which can be fine-tuned to maximize freshwater yield.
The method requires minimal energy compared to traditional desalination processes and can be implemented with relatively simple infrastructure.
Although it requires careful management of conditions to ensure optimal separation, this innovative approach provides a sustainable, low-cost solution for desalination.
Its simplicity and efficiency could make it a practical method for regions with limited freshwater resources and high energy costs
